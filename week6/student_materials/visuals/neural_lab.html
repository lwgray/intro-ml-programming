Key insight

ReLU always outputs zero or positive — it can never pass a negative value forward. The "slope flipping" you see in Case B happens because the weight after ReLU is negative, not because ReLU itself flips the sign.

This is what makes ReLU so useful: it creates a gating effect. A neuron either fires (positive output) or stays silent (zero). The network learns which neurons should fire for which inputs.

ReLU(x) = max(0, x)
output to next layer = ReLU(w·pixel + b) × w_next

Why this matters

This is the universal approximation theorem in action. A network with enough neurons can fit any continuous function — including the complex decision boundaries needed to tell a "3" from an "8".

Each neuron places its hinge at a different threshold and learns a different slope. Together they piece-wise approximate the target, like many straight lines tracing a curve.

The chain rule in plain English

Backprop asks: "how much did each weight contribute to the mistake?" It works backwards — output first, input last — multiplying the sensitivity at each step. That product is the gradient.

The gradient tells you which direction to nudge each weight. Multiply by the learning rate (a small number like 0.1) and subtract. Repeat thousands of times — that is training.

grad_v = error × sigmoid'(z_out) × h
grad_w = error × sigmoid'(z_out) × v × sigmoid'(z_hidden) × x
Reading the gradients

When the orange grad= labels appear on the connections, a positive gradient means "this weight pushed the output too high — reduce it." A negative gradient means "push it higher." The learning rate controls how big a step to take.

Reading the diagram

The top row is the forward pass — input x flows through weight w into the hidden neuron, then through weight v into the output. Each neuron computes sigmoid(w·x + b).

The bottom row is the backward pass — blame starts at the output, travels left through each weight. The gradient on each arrow tells you exactly how much that weight contributed to the mistake.

grad_v = (o − y) × sigmoid'(z_out) × h
grad_w = (o − y) × sigmoid'(z_out) × v × sigmoid'(z_hidden) × x

Connecting to the real network

The 0.5 influence values for salt and pepper are the weights — exactly the same as w and v in the backprop diagram. In a real network nobody chooses these values. They start random and gradient descent discovers the right numbers — which are different for every cuisine — through thousands of training rounds.

The 2 in the blame formula comes from differentiating the loss — when you square the mistake to get MSE, the power drops down as a 2 during the derivative. That is the chain rule at work.

blame on weight = 2 × mistake × (weight's influence on score)
new weight = old weight − (learning rate × blame)

Why this matters for real networks

A network trained on Chinese-American recipes learns completely different weights than one trained on American home cooking. Same architecture, same algorithm, same learning rate — but the data determines what the weights converge to.

This is why you cannot take a model trained on one task and expect it to work on a different task. The weights encode the patterns in the training data. Change the data, and you need to retrain from scratch — or fine-tune from the old weights.

Why depth matters

Each layer's receptive field grows. Layer 1 looks at a 5×5 patch of pixels — just enough for an edge. Layer 2 combines several edges into a loop or arc — a much larger region. By the output, the network "sees" the entire digit at once.

This is why deep networks outperform shallow ones: they build up reusable abstractions. An edge detector learned for "9" is also useful for "8", "6", and "0".

Connecting to backprop

Every arrow in the diagram is a weight. During training, the MSE loss from Lessons 3, 4, 5, and 6 flows backwards through all of these arrows simultaneously — adjusting thousands of edge detectors, shape detectors, and output connections in one pass.

That is exactly the chain rule from Lessons 3–6, applied at scale.
