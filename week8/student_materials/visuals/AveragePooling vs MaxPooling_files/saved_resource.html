Max says A wins (9 > 5): it can't tell one blazing pixel from a whole glowing patch — a shout is a shout, even if it's noise.

Average says B wins (5 > 2.25): consistent evidence beats a lone spike — but real, small detections get watered down too.

Neither is "right." They answer different questions: "did anyone find it?" vs "how much of it is here overall?"

3 · Who uses which

Mid-network: max won

Between conv layers you're hunting features — a whisker tip, an edge — and the strongest local response is the signal. Max keeps detections crisp; averaging blurs them. Standard choice since AlexNet.

The 1998 footnote

LeNet — the first famous CNN — actually used average pooling (it called it "subsampling"). Max took over when deep nets arrived and sharp feature detection started to matter more. AveragePooling2D((2,2)) still exists in Keras.

Plot twist: you already use it

GAP is average pooling stretched over the entire map — GlobalAveragePooling2D(). At the summary stage the question flips to "how much sleeve overall?", where graded, whole-map evidence beats one loud pixel that might be noise. Max hunts; average summarizes.

Max asks "did anyone shout?" — average asks "what did the room say overall?" Mid-network you want the shout (crisp detection); at the end you want the room's consensus — which is exactly why your networks MaxPool in the middle and GlobalAveragePool at the end.
