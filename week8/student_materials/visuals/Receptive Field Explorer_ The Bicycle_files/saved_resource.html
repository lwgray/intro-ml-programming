Object world. One window can hold an entire wheel plus frame plus pedal. One filter answers one question, so describing all of this takes many concept channels — which is exactly what GAP hands the classifier: one number per concept.

Why this forces channel growth

At 28×28, every window holds a fragment — an edge, an arc, a bit of tube. There are only so many distinct fragments in the world, so ~32 filters cover the vocabulary.

At 3×3, one window can hold an entire wheel, plus frame tubes, plus the crank — many simultaneous things. One filter answers one question (“is my pattern here?”), so describing everything in that big window takes far more filters. Resolution shrinks, vocabulary grows.

One nuance for precision: a feature-map cell doesn't literally store the pixels you see in the loupe — it stores one score per filter for that window (32 channels = 32 numbers per cell). The loupe shows the window; the filters decide what in it gets noticed. That's exactly why a richer window needs more scores.

Deep in the network, “one spot on the map” means “one big region of the image.” The map stops telling you where things are and starts telling you what is there — and saying what takes more channels.
