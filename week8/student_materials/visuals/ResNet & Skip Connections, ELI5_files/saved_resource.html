ResNet: The Skip Connection That Unlocked Depth

By 2015, everyone knew deeper networks should be better — but past ~20 layers they mysteriously got worse. ResNet's fix is one wire: let every block's input skip around it and get added back to its output. That wire took networks from 20 layers to 152.

1 · The problem

Deeper was worse — and not because of overfitting

This was the shocking part: the 56-layer network had worse training error than the 20-layer one. It wasn't memorizing too much — it couldn't even fit the training data. Depth was breaking optimization itself.

2 · The idea

Edit the draft — don't retype the document

A plain deep net retypes the whole representation at every layer: if layer 23 garbles it, everything downstream inherits the damage. A residual block instead receives a draft, computes a suggested edit F(x), and the skip wire carries the original draft around to be recombined: output = draft + edit.

The detour learns the change, not the content. Conv → BN → ReLU → Conv → BN (you already know every piece — BN lives inside each block) computes F(x): "here's what I'd improve about this draft."

The skip costs nothing and learns nothing. It's a wire, not a layer — 0 parameters. It just hands the original x to the ⊕.

The worst case is now harmless. If a block has nothing useful to add, it learns F(x) ≈ 0 and the draft passes through untouched. Learning "zero edit" is easy; learning "perfectly copy the input through 5 layers" (what plain nets needed) is hard. That's why adding blocks can't make things worse.

output = x + F(x) // keep the draft, add the edit — "residual" = the edit

Look familiar? Maps shrink, channels grow (56²×64 → 7²×512), and it ends with GAP → one number per concept channel → Dense — the same pattern as our Week 8 CNN, just repeated 8 blocks deep. At each stage boundary (↓2) the map halves and channels double, so the skip there uses a 1×1 conv to match shapes — the only skips with any weights at all.

4 · Why it trains

The skips are a gradient highway

Training feedback must travel backward from the loss to layer 1. Through a plain stack it fades a little at every layer — after 50+ layers, almost nothing arrives. But the ⊕ passes gradient straight through the skip, so feedback rides the arcs past every block:

5 · Why it mattered
2012

AlexNet · 8 layers

15.3% top-5 error. The ImageNet moment — your Segment 2 story.

2014

VGG · 19 layers

7.3%. All 3×3 filters. Tried going deeper — hit the wall.

2015

ResNet · 152 layers

3.6% — past the ~5% human benchmark. One wire unlocked 8× the depth.

A residual block promises: "at worst, I'll do nothing." Give every layer an escape hatch to the identity, and depth stops being a risk — gradients ride the skips backward, drafts ride them forward, and 152 layers train as easily as 20.
