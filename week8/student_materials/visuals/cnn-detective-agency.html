The Detective Agency

A convolutional neural network, told as a building full of investigators who never see the crime scene — and still name the suspect. Everything below runs on real arithmetic: the numbers you'll see are an actual CNN forward pass on the actual image.

One case runs top to bottom: a 12×12 photo comes in the front door, and a verdict gets stamped on the roof.

Exhibit A · The scene — input & RGB channels

One scene, three lamps

The crime scene is documented three times: once under a red lamp, once under a green lamp, once under a blue lamp. Three brightness maps of the same grid, stacked. That's all "RGB channels" means — not three pictures, but three aligned measurements of every square.

every square holds 3 numbers, one per lamp.

The tensor: 12 × 12 × 3. Height, width, depth = number of lamps. Hold that word "depth" — it's about to matter.

Exhibit B · The field agent — convolution

One checklist, 27 boxes, one gut score

A field agent works one specialty. She carries a checklist that covers a 3×3 patch of squares — but each square has three lamp readings, so the checklist has 3×3×3 = 27 weighted boxes. At each spot: multiply every reading by its weight, add it all up, write down one number. That single number is her gut score for "my clue is here."

Then she takes one step and does it again. Same checklist, every square of the scene. She doesn't learn a new checklist for the left side of the room — that reuse is called weight sharing, and it's why her whole specialty costs 27 numbers no matter how big the scene is.

She isn't smart. The agent has no idea what an "edge" is. The checklist is pure multiply-and-add. Everything that looks like judgment in this building is arithmetic wearing a trench coat.
Exhibit C · The whole floor — 32 channels out

32 agents → the scene becomes 32 overlays

The ground floor employs 32 specialties. Each walks the same scene with a different 27-box checklist and produces her own pin map. Stack the pin maps and look at what you have:

…plus 28 more specialties not shown. Output tensor: 12 × 12 × 32.

The recursion — this is the whole trick. The scene arrived as a grid with 3 overlays. It leaves the ground floor as a grid with 32 overlays. Same shape of thing. So the second-floor analysts do exactly the job the first floor did — 3×3 loupe, one weighted checklist, one gut score — except their checklists have 3×3×32 = 288 boxes, and they're reading pin maps, not the scene. They detect clues about clues. Nobody above the ground floor ever sees the photo. Every floor of this building is the same floor.

And that's why an analyst's reach grows without her loupe growing: her 3×3 window sits on nine summaries that each covered 3×3 of the scene, overlapping into 5×5. Loupe stays tiny; secondhand coverage compounds every floor. That reach has a name — the receptive field.

Exhibit D · Agency policy — ReLU

"We don't file negative reports"

Agency rule: if your evidence points against your clue, you don't file "−6 slanted-line-ness." You file zero. Positive findings pass through untouched; everything below zero is flattened.

Red-ink squares are negative scores — currently filed as-is.

Why throw information away? Because without this rule, the building collapses. Weighted sums of weighted sums are algebraically just one big weighted sum — stack fifty floors of pure checklists and you've built one giant ground-floor checklist, no hierarchy at all. The refusal to pass negatives along is the nonlinearity that makes floor two genuinely different from floor one. Depth means something only because of this rule.
Exhibit E · The calibration desk — batch norm

Grading the agents on a curve

Problem: Agent 11 is dramatic — everything's a 9. Agent 04 understates — a 2 from her is huge news. Raw scores from different specialties aren't comparable, and worse, each agent's temperament drifts while the agency is still in training. Every desk upstairs would waste its time re-learning everyone's personality, over and over.

So between the agents and the "no negatives" policy sits the calibration desk. Each training day the agency processes a batch of cases. For each specialty, the desk collects every score that specialty produced that day — every square, every case — and grades on a curve: subtract the day's average, divide by the day's spread. Now "one unit" means the same amount of surprise from every agent.

The two dials are learned, one pair per specialty. Without them, the curve would force every agent to be equally loud forever — but some clues genuinely matter more. γ and β hand the volume decision back to training, while keeping the standardized starting point that makes learning fast and stable.

Two consequences worth their own line. First: agents add no personal baseline to their reports (bias=False in the conv) — the desk subtracts the mean, so any constant they added would vanish anyway; β is where the baseline lives now. Second: on deployment day there's one case and no class to curve against — so the desk uses its historical averages from training. That's the real difference between a network's train mode and eval mode.
Exhibit F · Consolidation — stride & channel growth

Fewer desks, more specialties

Going upstairs, the agency consolidates: the next floor sets up desks at every other corner only — half the rows, half the columns, a quarter of the desks. And at exactly that moment, it doubles the specialties.

Why more specialties higher up? There are only so many kinds of raw marks — a smudge is a smudge; 32 checklists cover the street level. But combinations of marks explode: smudge-next-to-fiber, fur-texture-with-dark-spot, curve-meeting-curve. Each floor's vocabulary has to grow because it's describing arrangements of the floor below.

Why it's affordable: a quarter of the desks, but each desk's checklist covers twice the incoming specialties and there are twice as many desk-types — 4× the cost per corner. Quarter × four = flat payroll per floor. The agency isn't getting bigger going up; it's trading positions for vocabulary. Downstairs knows precisely where and barely what; upstairs knows richly what and only roughly where.

Exhibit G · The review board — global average pooling

One number per specialist, location destroyed on purpose

By the top floor the scene is a coarse 3×3 grid with 128 overlays of high-level findings. The review board doesn't ask "where exactly was the fur texture?" It asks "in this case, overall — how much fur texture?" So for each specialty it takes the average of the whole map: one number per specialist.

…and 120 more. 3×3×128 → 128 numbers.

Two things GAP buys. Whether the scene was a studio apartment or a warehouse, the board always receives exactly 128 numbers — the network stops caring about input size. And it saves a fortune: wiring every desk position to every verdict (flatten → dense layer) is where old architectures like VGG buried ~90% of their 138M parameters. Averaging first makes that wall of wiring unnecessary.
Exhibit H · The chief — the final linear layer

The only place "dog" exists

The chief receives the board's summary — 128 numbers — and holds one weighting per possible verdict. Multiply, add, loudest verdict wins. Note what's not in the building: no analyst has "dog" on her badge. Fur-tone fires for dogs, cats, and camel coats. Dark-spots fires for eyes, buttons, and bolt heads. "Dog" is a chord — a particular combination of specialists lighting up together — and the chief's weights are the only place that chord is written down.

Case closed · Who writes the checklists?

Nobody

On day one, every checklist in the building is random noise and the chief's verdicts are garbage. But each verdict gets graded against an answer key, and blame flows back down the org chart — every desk finds out how much its boxes contributed to the mistake, and nudges them. A few million graded cases later, the ground floor has congealed into edge detectors and the upper floors into texture and part detectors. Nobody designed that; it's the residue of grading.

This is the actual historical divide: computer vision before 2012 was people hand-writing the checklists (SIFT, HOG). Deep learning's move was letting the checklists be the thing that learns.

Questions your students will ask
"Why don't the deep pin maps look like the dog?"
Three independent reasons, any one sufficient. (1) A top-floor map is a 3×3 grid — sixteen-ish numbers can't depict anything; the picture-ness was traded away floors ago, on purpose, for vocabulary. (2) It's a scorecard, not a photo — "amount of fur-texture around here: 8" is the answer to a question, and answers don't resemble their subjects. (3) There's no dog unit — dog is the chord across all 128 summaries; any single map is one note. Bonus: the famous "layer 5 sees faces" images are feature visualizations — synthetic inputs optimized to maximize one unit — i.e., that unit's fantasy, not anything it ever saw.
"Why 27 boxes and not 9?"
A filter's depth always equals its input's depth — it must weigh every incoming overlay, and its per-lamp findings are summed into one score, not kept separate. That's why cross-channel checklists exist at all: the fur-tone agent only works because she can reward red-lamp readings while penalizing blue-lamp readings. (It's also why people write "3×3" and never "3×3×3" — the last dimension is forced.)
"Why does the number of specialties stay the same between two floors in a row, then jump?"
Doubling is tied to consolidation, not to floor count. The invariant is flat payroll: cost per floor ≈ positions × incoming specialties × outgoing specialties × 9. Halving each spatial side cuts positions 4×; doubling channels on both ends multiplies the middle 4×. They cancel — so you double exactly when you stride, and never otherwise. Consecutive same-width floors are buying reach (receptive field) and extra nonlinearity, not vocabulary.
"What does the calibration desk do with a single case at test time?"
There's no batch to curve against, so it applies the running mean and variance accumulated during training. This is what model.eval() actually switches. Related honest caveat: during training the curve depends on who else is in the batch — the same image normalizes slightly differently in different company, and tiny batches make the curve noisy. That's a real BN weakness, not a flaw in the analogy.
"Who decided the ground floor should detect edges?"
Nobody — see Case Closed above. Worth telling them that pre-2012 vision really did hand-author the checklists (SIFT/HOG), which makes "the features are learned" land as a historical event rather than a slogan.
Where the analogy leaks — say these out loud

Agents seem intelligent. They're 27 multiplications and an addition. Repeat this until it's boring; anthropomorphizing filters is the #1 residue this analogy leaves.

Checklists seem authored. Every checklist in a trained net is learned residue, not design. The analogy's noun ("checklist") quietly implies an author — the training section is the antidote.
